    Though the average range for uric acid is often given in the area you suggest, the top end is really too high for someone who has had gout, and 350µmol/L (6mg/dL) is the best target for gout sufferers. This will stop new urate crystals forming, and will start to dissolve old crystals. To get old urate deposits to dissolve faster, it is good to aim much lower for a few months, maybe as low as 200, but you should discuss this with his doctor first.

    Allopurinol begins to work almost immediately, but the full effect on blood uric acid is reached over 2 to 3 days. As you increased dosage a week ago, the latest test will show a reasonable result reflecting the effect of 200mg dose.

    You and Dad need to be aware that, until all old crystals are dissolved, there is still a risk of gout attacks. This gets less and less as time goes by, but Dad will still need pain relief if he cannot stand the pain. I have no qualifications to advise you what the best pain relief is, but if you cannot get a clear recommendation from his doctor, it is best to ask a pharmacist, making sure they know about the ulcer history.

        Hi Nina,

        You must understand that 6.7mg/dL is borderline safe. It can easily lead to a horrible situation where the uric acid from old crystals dissolving raise the uric acid to the point where new crystals start forming. As I have said, you can never be sure that you will not get a gout attack until all crystals have gone, but at 6.7, you are not reducing the risk by much.       Hi Jeff,

      I have shared your thoughts on gout medication for life, then the truth hit me. I was committing myself to a lifetime of ibuprofen and other pain control. Read your packet notes for warnings about longterm NSAIDs dangers.

      Pain relief does nothing to stop uric acid crystals destroying your joints, so I came to my senses, and talked with my doctors about allopurinol. Gout experts have differing views on what is safe. Some say 6mg/dL (0.36mmol/L). More conservative rheumatologists argue for 5mg/dL, as it gives a better safety margin against natural fluctuations of uric acid in the blood. I prefer the 5mg/dL upper limit, but it is a matter of personal choice. However, whichever you decide on, there are two important considerations:

      1. In the first few months of treatment, it is good to aim well below 5. This is because you have lots of old uric acid crystals all around your body, and the sooner you get rid of them, the better. For this reason, I recommend at least six months on 900mg allopurinol per day. http://www.goutpal.com/3026/where-is-your-gout-going/

      2. Allopurinol dose in itself is absolutely not important. What matters is the effect on your uric acid level. If 200mg gets you safe, then that is the right dose. If you need 400mg to maintain uric acid at 5mg/dL (0.30mmol/L), then that is the right dose. A 180lb guy with a uric acid level of 12mg/dL may well need a higher dose than a 350lb guy with 8mg/dL. One size does *not* fit all.

      That’s good timing, John. I was just looking at reformatting an old page on “stopping allopurinol” http://www.goutpal.com/forum/gout-support/doc-says-to-stop-allopurinol-due-to-gout-flare-help/

      Are you in the same situation as Jenny’s husband? Poor advice from the doctor and worried about stopping allopurinol during a gout flare?

      The short answer is do not stop allopurinol once you have started it.

      There might be exceptional circumstances, but most flares during allopurinol therapy are a result of old crystals melting. You might get a little pain from that, but the last thing you want to do is stop that process. Pain from dissolving crystals is very short lived, and once those crystals have gone, they’ll never trouble you again.

      If you stop the allopurinol, you stop the crystals dissolving. Your immune system will attack them again, potentially causing another gout flare. Those undissolved uric acid crystals will still be hanging around to cause more gout pain in future.

      I can see that you are reluctant to visit your doctor to discuss side effects, but you really must do. First, you must check with him that the blood pressure medicine is gout-safe. There are many types of blood pressure treatments. Some help gout treatments, but some make gout worse.

Make sure you get the exact numbers from the test results, then come back here and discuss them.
